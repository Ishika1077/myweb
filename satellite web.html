Satellite

A satellite is an object that orbits around another object in space. There are two main types:

  • Natural satellites: like the Moon orbiting Earth.
  • Artificial satellites: human-made devices launched into space to orbit Earth or other celestial bodies.

Why Do We Use Satellites?

We use satellites because they provide critical services that support modern life in ways that are impossible or inefficient to achieve from the ground. Satellites enable global communication by transmitting television, phone, and internet signals across continents, especially to remote or rural areas without traditional infrastructure. They power navigation systems like GPS, which are essential for transportation, logistics, mapping, and everyday use in smartphones. Satellites also play a vital role in weather forecasting, helping meteorologists monitor atmospheric conditions, track storms, and provide early warnings for natural disasters. In addition, Earth observation satellites help us monitor environmental changes, such as deforestation, pollution, and climate change, offering data that supports agriculture, disaster management, and conservation efforts. Scientific satellites explore space, study celestial objects, and gather information about the universe, while military and intelligence satellites are used for national security, surveillance, and secure communication. Overall, satellites are indispensable tools that connect the world, enhance safety, upport science, and help manage and protect the planet.

How Do Satellite Solar Panels Work At Long Distances?

Earth stationed solar panels (solar shields) have to contend with Earth’s unique elements like dust, rain, snow, hail, clouds, thermal changes, and other weathering. In space, these elements are absent, generating a benefit seven times more kilowatt hours per year from a single photovoltaic solar panel. Only about 60% of sunlight energy manages to get through all the organic obstacles on its way to the terrestrial surface. Satellite solar panels (SSP) would work no different than the Earth-based shields but clearly have fewer blockades to encounter. With the restrictions of Earth and its atmospheres, satellite solar shields maintain broader freedom to follow the sun’s direction for maximum absorption of sunlight. The panels also have the ability to contract and expand for even greater sun absorption. SSPs naturally gather solar radiation 24 hours a day nonstop, reflecting enormous amounts of boundless energy on giant mirrors, which bounce onto small solar collectors. That power gets efficiently converted into infrared lasers. These lasers transmit 80% of the collected energy to 10,000 square-foot Earthbound receivers. If an atmospheric incident, like clouds, interrupts the beam, the satellite would redirect the pulse to other receivers on the network.

How Do We Use Satellites?

We use satellites by placing them into specific orbits around the Earth to perform various tasks, depending on their purpose. Satellites are launched into space using powerful rockets and positioned in orbits such as low Earth orbit (LEO), medium Earth orbit (MEO), or geostationary orbit (GEO). Once in orbit, they are controlled and monitored by ground stations on Earth, which send instructions and receive data. Satellites use onboard instruments and sensors to collect information, such as images of Earth, weather patterns, or signals for communication and navigation. This data is then transmitted back to Earth using radio waves, where it is processed and used for applications like weather forecasting, GPS navigation, internet services, television broadcasting, and scientific research. Some satellites also work together in constellations to provide continuous global coverage, like those used for global internet or GPS systems. Through this process, satellites serve as reliable tools to observe, connect, and support life on Earth.
